# SPLITDEV — Deep UX/UI Audit

**Source of truth:** the May 16 build of Splitdev1 / Writers Atelier as captured in the latest screenshot. Spanish production script (45 scenes, p. 3/17, Blue draft), Tier 1/2/3 shipped. Active scene: **7. INT. HACIENDA SERRANO — ESTUDIO — DÍA**.

This is not a list of nits. It is the design doc for the next phase — what's working, what's broken, and the principles that turn this from a great-looking screenwriting app into the **power tool** for screenplay development.

---

## 1 — What's working (don't break it)

The build has earned real ground. Before we touch it, name what it does that nothing else does.

1. **The Beat Sheet ribbon** (T3.1A) is the strongest first-impression any writing tool gives. Three acts colored, Save-the-Cat beats anchored above scene ticks, the active scene's `SC 7` pill copper-stamped on the rail. **A screenwriter knows where they are in three seconds.** No other screenwriting app communicates structure this fast.
2. **The location-grouped sidebar** (T3.2A). Twelve "GRUPO SERRANO" repeats collapsed to one expandable header with eleven scenes underneath. Sub-locations rendered as Courier (`PISO EJECUTIVO · DÍA`, `OFICINA DE DOÑA CARMEN · DÍA`). This is professional-screenwriter ergonomics.
3. **Industry-grade chrome.** Diagonal slate stripes in the top corners, copper accent rail under the topbar, `BLUE DRAFT` pill with the right blue, `45 scenes` quietly under the title, `Saved` indicator (T1.7) — every detail says *production tool*, not *startup MVP*.
4. **The quiet notes column** (T2.3). "On this scene — Nothing pinned here yet" then "8 OPEN / 0 DISCUSSING / 0 APPLIED" then collapsed "All notes (8) showing everything." The empty-state-as-default is exactly right.
5. **`INGEST NOTES`.** As a feature label, this is the kill shot. A writer pastes the producer's email, the tool parses it into structured notes with origin/priority/scene. Nothing else in the category does this. **Build the demo around this.**
6. **The "ADDRESS YOUR MESSAGE TO" agent rail** at the bottom of the notes column. Replacing the WhatsApp chat with a single compose box that you *address* to one of six agents is a major upgrade.
7. **`♪ CAST`** instead of "Read Aloud." Three characters of better naming.
8. **`+ NEW NOTE`** as a bold copper CTA — the right visual weight for the most common action on the right rail.
9. **Page counter `p. 3 / 17`** with the right grammar — fraction over total, not eighths fluff in the top bar.
10. The numbered scene heading (`7. INT. HACIENDA SERRANO — ESTUDIO — DÍA`) is correctly proportioned and the copper underline rule below it is a screenplay-aware detail most tools miss.

This is a serious build. Now to the work.

## 2 — Bugs visible in this screenshot (must-fix before anything else)

These are showstoppers. Any one of them, on a producer demo, costs the tool credibility.

### 2.1 — Dialogue text is being mashed by whitespace collapse

Look at the page. Count the broken words:

- `Tenemos quehablarde Grupo Serrano.` → should be *que hablar de*
- `Si fuera por Benjamín, ya estaríaenterrada.` → should be *estaría enterrada*
- `Quéidiota eres.` → should be *Qué idiota*
- `El sillón depielde la abuela domina el espacio.` → should be *de piel de*
- `Huele a…"hambre".` → the curly quotes are wrong direction

This looks like a parser/renderer issue (likely whitespace collapsing across line breaks in the fountain → HTML pipeline, or a tokenizer that's eating spaces around punctuation). **It is the single most damaging bug in the app.** A screenwriter cannot trust a tool that mangles their words on screen. Fix this before the demo.

### 2.2 — `GRUPO SERRANO` and `Grupo serrano` are grouped as two different locations

Sidebar shows `GRUPO SERRANO (11)` near the top and `Grupo serrano (1)` near the bottom. The location-grouping helper is case-sensitive when it shouldn't be. Normalize to upper-case canonical form (and trim trailing whitespace, em-dash variants, accent normalization) before grouping. Also: `HACIENDA SERRANO — RECÁMARA DE DO…` is being grouped under itself instead of under `HACIENDA SERRANO` because the sub-location is part of the group key. The grouping should always cut at the first `—` (em-dash) or ` - ` separator.

### 2.3 — Beat ribbon labels are truncated at both edges

Top of ribbon reads `…ING IMAGEHEME STATED CATALYST DEBATE BREAK INTO TWO FUN & GAMES MIDPOINT BAD GUYS CLOSE IN ALL IS LOSTDARK NIGHBREAK INTO THREE FINALE FINAL IMA`. The first beat (`OPENING IMAGE`) collides with the second (`THEME STATED`) at the left edge; `ALL IS LOST` and `DARK NIGHT` collide; `BREAK INTO THREE` is mashed; the final `FINAL IMAGE` is cut to `FINAL IMA`. **At 45 scenes, the ribbon is already too dense for all 13 beats.** The spec said hide minor beats when scene count > 60; the real threshold is much lower. Two fixes layered:
- Auto-hide minor beats below a viewport-width threshold (calculate based on label-width math, not scene count).
- The first and last beat labels need padding off the rail edges — let the act zones bleed past, but pull labels in 12px from the edge.

### 2.4 — Text bleeding behind the ribbon

Look just below the bottom of the beat ribbon, around `SC 7 · p. 3`: there's faint Spanish text peeking through (`…mente extraídas allí. Importante crece las verdes selvas de…`). The script is scrolling *under* the ribbon instead of being clipped by the topbar's bottom edge. The script container needs `position: relative; z-index: 0` and the ribbon `z-index: 5`, or the script gets a top padding equal to ribbon height. As-is, the script appears to glitch.

### 2.5 — Active scene isn't visible in the sidebar

The current scene is `7. HACIENDA SERRANO — ESTUDIO — DÍA`. The sidebar is scrolled to show scenes 11–42, with `HACIENDA SERRANO — RECÁMARA DE DO…` collapsed at the bottom. **The active scene's group is off-screen.** When the user clicks a scene from the timeline ribbon (or the script auto-scrolls a new scene into view), the sidebar should expand the matching group and reveal the active row. Currently it doesn't.

### 2.6 — Two CTAs for the same action

`+ NEW NOTE` button (copper, top-right) and `Right-click a line → Note this` (empty-state hint). Same verb, two routes, slightly different metaphors. Pick one: I'd retire `+ NEW NOTE` and lean fully on selection-driven note creation (right-click or `⌘⇧N` on selection). The empty-state hint becomes the only call to action because **a note without an anchor isn't a useful note**. (More on this in §3.)

### 2.7 — `Ask AI` floating next to the scene heading

A copper-bordered chip with a star: `★ Ask AI`. Where does it go? What does it ask? On a slug line, "Ask AI" is a verb without an object. Either kill it or make it specific (`★ Ask Structure about this scene` — and only if that's the one verb worth elevating to a permanent UI element).

### 2.8 — The `S` margin badge floats with no anchor

A small copper-on-blue `S` chip sits in the right margin around the third visible line of scene 7. It's presumably a Structure agent pin. But it's vertically un-aligned with any specific line and it has no body text — what is it pinned to? Either move it to align with its anchor line precisely, or hide it until hover/click reveals the pinned content.

### 2.9 — The inline `— PAGE 3 —` divider is noisy

In the middle of scene 7, a copper-italicized `— PAGE 3 —` separator interrupts the read. Real screenplays put page numbers in the upper-right corner only and don't break the flow with a page label. The existing right-margin `p. 2` indicator is the right home for this signal. Kill the inline divider.

### 2.10 — Title is `Untitled` but the project has 45 scenes

The topbar says `Untitled / 45 scenes`. A 45-scene project that's still called Untitled is a UX failure of the file model. On first save, prompt for a title (`THE CABIN`, `EL FUTURO`, whatever). The Untitled state should not persist past first ingest.

## 3 — The IA diagnosis: what's structurally still wrong

Bugs are easy. Below are the deeper UX-architecture issues — the things that no amount of polish will fix because they're load-bearing wrong.

### 3.1 — The page is still a column, not the canvas

At the screenshot's viewport width (~1620px), the script is ~750px wide between sidebar (~285px) and notes column (~360px). That's roughly **46% of viewport** dedicated to the actual writing surface. The other 54% is chrome.

A real screenwriting app reverses this. **The page is the canvas; everything else is summoned.** Final Draft, Highland 2, WriterDuet, Fade In — none of them give equal real estate to a notes column and a script column. The script is centered, full-width, and panels slide *over* or *beside* it on demand.

Today's three-column layout is the single biggest structural fix left.

**Fix:** notes column and sidebar both become slide-out rails (`⌘1` for sidebar, `⌘2` for notes). Default state on a fresh load: page only, beat ribbon, timeline, command bar. Writer is one keystroke from any panel and one `⌘.` from solitude.

### 3.2 — Three navigation surfaces compete for the same job

The user can navigate scenes via:
- **Sidebar** (location-grouped tree, scene numbers, note counts)
- **Beat ribbon** (act zones, beat names, scene ticks, active `SC` pill)
- **Page scroll** (continuous)

That's *three* navigation models for one task. Each is good. Together they cost cognitive overhead — the writer has to decide *which* nav to use for which trip. Solution: the beat ribbon and the sidebar should be **two facets of one navigator**, both responding to the same active-scene state, both auto-following each other. The user picks whichever is closer to their cursor; they always agree.

Specifically: clicking a tick on the beat ribbon should expand the matching sidebar group and highlight the scene row. And vice versa.

### 3.3 — The agent compose dock is a chat panel with extra steps

"ADDRESS YOUR MESSAGE TO: [Structure] [Dialogue] [Character] [Horror] [Conflict] [Theme]" — six chips. Pick one. Type message. Send.

This is conceptually still a chat. The improvement vs. v1 is real (single compose, no thread history bloat). But the metaphor is unchanged: *writer sends message, agent replies*. As a screenwriter I rarely want to *type a question* to an agent. I want to:

- **Mark a line as broken** (select → ⌘R → see three rewrites)
- **Find every line that does X** (select sample → "find similar")
- **Flag a tic** (right-click → "tag as on-the-nose")
- **Diagnose a beat** (select scene heading → "is this scene paying off setup?")

Each of these is a verb on a selection, not a sentence in a chat box. The compose dock should be the *fallback* — when the writer wants to ask something the verbs don't cover. It should not be the primary input.

**Fix:** demote the agent dock; promote selection + command palette (`⌘K`) as the main input. See §4.3.

### 3.4 — Lenses are absent

Six agents currently behave like contacts you can address. They should also behave like **lenses you toggle on the page**. With the Dialogue lens on, every line that breaks a Bible voice rule gets a copper margin diamond. With Horror lens on, weak dread moments get a margin glyph. With Structure lens on, the beat ribbon gets weak-setup/payoff underlines.

The shipped product has six agents and no lens model. That's the largest unrealized capability in the build today.

### 3.5 — Voice rules sit inert in the Bible

In the prototype data, Sarah's rule is *"Asks questions instead of stating fear."* It's a beautiful insight. It currently lives in a drawer. **It should live on the page** — when she says `"I'm scared"` the line gets a tiny mark in the margin: *"breaks rule: asks questions about fear, doesn't name it."*

Bible voice rules → active extractors → page margins. This is how the Bible stops being a reference document and starts being an editing instrument.

### 3.6 — There is no cursor in the page

Can't tell from the screenshot alone, but every indicator in the visible UI — `+ NEW NOTE`, `Ask AI`, `INGEST NOTES`, `Right-click → Note this`, an agent dock for composing about lines rather than writing them — suggests the writer is in a **review stance** on the script, not a **writing stance**. If the script is genuinely typeable, the UI doesn't communicate that. If it isn't, that's the biggest missing piece in the entire roadmap.

A writer must be able to:
- Click into a line, type, Backspace, navigate with arrows.
- Press `Tab` to cycle screenplay elements (Action → Character → Dialogue → Parenthetical → Action).
- Press `Enter` to break to next element with the correct formatting.

This is non-negotiable. Without it, SPLITDEV is a *review tool* permanently, no matter how good the lenses and pins get.

### 3.7 — The timeline-as-history is missing

The beat ribbon is a **position** surface (where am I in the structure?). It is not a **history** surface (what changed when?). Yet the topbar carries `BLUE DRAFT` and `Saved` — implying there's history. There needs to be a second strip that's the **revision timeline**: pins for each color (White, Blue, Pink…), draggable playhead, named milestones ("Producer notes — May 12"), scrubbing diff. Either give the beat ribbon a second row for revisions, or add a second 28px strip beneath it.

### 3.8 — The notes column shows too many counting concepts at once

Look at the right rail. In one panel:

1. Header: `Notes` / `Scene 7 · 0 pinned`
2. Two CTAs: `INGEST NOTES` + `+ NEW NOTE`
3. Tabs: `Notes 8` / `Patterns 0`
4. Empty state: `Nothing pinned here yet. Right-click a line → Note this`
5. Tri-counter: `8 OPEN / 0 DISCUSSING / 0 APPLIED`
6. Disclosure: `▸ ALL NOTES (8) showing everything`
7. Agent dock: `Structure Agent — PLOT, PACING, ACT BREAKS / BIBLE / ★0`
8. Agent rail: `ADDRESS YOUR MESSAGE TO: Structure | Dialogue | Character | Horror | Conflict | Theme`
9. Compose box: `Compose to Structure`

**Nine sections in a 360px column.** Each is justifiable in isolation. Together they're a wall. The screenwriter has to scan past 8 things to find the 1 thing they want.

Aggressive trim plan, with no functional loss:
- Combine (3) and (5) and (6) into a single block: `Notes 8 / Patterns 0` with a 3-dot legend underneath (`● 8 open · ● 0 discussing · ● 0 done`). The disclosure becomes implicit.
- Demote (4) to dimmer state when the user has notes.
- Move (7)+(8)+(9) into a *separate* floating panel (see §4.3) summoned by `⌘K` or by clicking an agent's lens.

The notes column shrinks to: header → scene-pinned list (or empty state) → all-notes list (below a thin rule). Two regions. The end.

### 3.9 — `Cards` view is a sibling of `Script`, not a layer

The topbar offers `SCRIPT | CARDS` as mutually exclusive states. But cards and script aren't different products — they're different *layouts* over the same data. The right model: `⌘\` toggles corkboard layer; the script-state (active scene, cursor position, scroll) is preserved underneath. Coming back from cards lands you exactly where you left.

### 3.10 — There is no Reading mode visible

`⌘\` per the Tier 3 spec opens full-bleed paper. The topbar shows `SCRIPT | CARDS | ♪ CAST | EXPORT` — no Read icon, no obvious keystroke. The single highest-impact demo state needs a button-shaped presence in the spine.

## 4 — Six redesign moves (the principle-level changes)

These are the structural changes that turn the current build into an actual power tool. Each is shippable on its own.

### 4.1 — Make the page primary

**Move:** convert sidebar and notes column from always-on to slide-out. Default load = page + ribbon + timeline + command bar. `⌘1` slides in sidebar; `⌘2` slides in notes; `⌘.` is solitude.

The script grows to ~960px wide centered, with 220px of right margin reserved permanently for **the annotation gutter** (line-aligned pins). The gutter is always visible. The notes column is the *editor* of the gutter, not its display.

**Why this matters:** every other principle below depends on the page being primary. If the page is one of three columns, the lenses don't have room to mark up; the corkboard can't take over; reading mode is a half-step.

### 4.2 — The annotation gutter is the only home for marginalia

Today's notes column hosts notes. The script's right margin hosts page numbers, asterisks, and that floating `S` badge. These are the same surface, modeled twice.

**Move:** unify. Every marginal signal lives in **one 220px gutter to the right of the page**, line-aligned:

- Revision asterisks (existing, T1)
- Agent pins (Structure `S`, Dialogue `D`, etc.) — currently the floating `S`
- Voice-rule violations (new — copper diamonds, hover for the rule)
- Note pins, color-coded by origin (Producer purple, Exec copper, Director blue, Reader green, Self grey)
- Pattern pins (the killer — small `Tic·4` glyphs)
- Linked-scene markers (currently the `LINKED NOTE` slugline badge)

The right column ("Notes") becomes a *list/editor* on the same model — it's a vertical projection of the gutter. Click a gutter pin → that note expands in the gutter and the right-column scrolls to it. Right-column is the keyboardable editor; gutter is the spatial truth.

### 4.3 — Selection + command palette is the input grammar

**Move:** every action that's currently a button or chip somewhere in the chrome also exists as a verb in a command bar at the bottom of the screen.

Trigger: `⌘K` from anywhere. The bar focuses. Type a verb. Done.

Commands (full grammar):

| Verb | Example | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| (plain text) | `tighten this opening` | Sends to last-used agent (or Structure default) about current selection / current line |
| `/dialogue` | `/dialogue rewrite as subtext` | Routes to Dialogue agent specifically |
| `/structure`, `/character`, `/horror`, `/conflict`, `/theme` | … | Same, per agent |
| `/find` | `/find suddenly` | Highlights all instances on the timeline + opens gutter list |
| `/note` | `/note benjamín on the nose` | Pins a self-note to selection or current line |
| `/only`, `/with`, `/without` | `/only karla`, `/with karla benjamín`, `/without isabela` | Composable character filters (currently mutually exclusive chips) |
| `/jump`, `p.`, `sc` | `p. 14`, `sc 7`, `b: midpoint` | Navigate to page / scene / beat |
| `/read` | `/read karla` | Cast read of current scene, optionally filtered to one character |
| `/pin` | `/pin "after producer notes"` | Drop a named milestone on the revision timeline |
| `/ingest` | `/ingest` | Open the ingest-notes flow |
| `/solitude`, `/corkboard`, `/reading` | … | Layout switches |

This single bar replaces the agent dock, the filter chips, the page count's clickability, the keyboard shortcuts you can't remember, and most of the right-rail navigation. Beginners discover commands by clicking through the GUI (every chip click shows what command it would have run). Power users live in the bar.

**Specifically demote:** the `ADDRESS YOUR MESSAGE TO:` agent rail. It still exists as a fallback for users who haven't learned `⌘K`, but it shrinks to a dim subtle row.

### 4.4 — Agents become lenses

**Move:** every agent has a toggleable lens. When on, it marks up the page silently.

Lens shelves live as 6 small dots in the spine — one per agent — colored. Click a dot to toggle. Multiple on = stacked margin signals.

| Lens | What it adds to the page |
|---|---|
| **Dialogue** on | Bible-voice-rule violations get copper diamonds in the gutter, line-aligned. Hover: rule citation. |
| **Structure** on | Beat ribbon shows weak setup/payoff underlines on scene ticks. Long Act II midpoints flag. |
| **Character** on | Scenes where the protagonist's WANT/NEED hasn't moved get a small grey `○` margin glyph. |
| **Horror** on | Action lines that overuse "suddenly", "without warning", "out of nowhere" get a faint red highlight. Dread-density per scene visualizes as a heat bar in the gutter. |
| **Conflict** on | Scenes with flat stakes flagged. Scenes that escalate stakes get a rising-arrow margin glyph. |
| **Theme** on | Motif occurrences (water, isolation, voicemail) tagged with a small glyph; recurring motifs get colored. |

**This is the conversion from "chatbot in a panel" to "annotation engine on the page."** The agents stop being people to message and start being readers leaning over the writer's shoulder. The chat panel survives only as a fallback verb in the command bar.

### 4.5 — Patterns get topbar status

The `Patterns 0` tab is buried in the right column. With 0 it's invisible. With non-zero it's still buried.

**Move:** patterns get a topbar pill. `◉ 3 patterns` sits in the topbar between page count and `Saved`. Click → command bar fills with `/patterns`. Press enter → timeline lights up with pattern hotspots; gutter floods with pattern pins; the page scrolls to the first one.

This is the single feature SPLITDEV has that no competitor touches. **Hide it and you've thrown away your differentiator. Surface it and the demo writes itself.**

### 4.6 — The bottom strip is the revision timeline

Currently: nothing exists below the script.

**Move:** a 32px strip at the bottom of the viewport that is the revision timeline. It carries:

- A playhead at the current revision state (default: head).
- Colored revision pins along its length: White → Blue → Pink → Yellow → Green → Goldenrod → Buff → Salmon. Each pin is a snapshot.
- Named milestones, dropped by the user with `/pin`: *"Producer notes"*, *"First read-through"*, *"Sent to lit manager"*.
- Note density as a thin baseline (1px tall amber bars below the rail) — replicating the existing per-scene chart, but spread across time.
- Drag the playhead → script morphs to that revision (typewriter-reveal of diff against current). Drop the playhead between two pins → "Continue from here" prompt → branches.

This kills the `BLUE DRAFT` dropdown in the topbar (replaced by "head pin = Blue" on the timeline) and the implicit history that was nowhere visible.

## 5 — Three flagship interactions (the demos)

If you can show these three in 90 seconds, the product sells itself.

### Demo 1 — "Ingest the producer's email, watch the script light up"

1. Producer just sent a 600-word email with five notes mixed into a paragraph.
2. Writer clicks `INGEST NOTES`. Paste. (Or `/ingest`.)
3. Tool parses: "I think the opening drags" → high-priority Structure note on Scene 1. "Karla's voice in the midpoint feels like Benjamín's" → Dialogue note on scenes 14, 16. "The hacienda visit is the wrong place for this turn" → Structure note on scene 21.
4. **The script's right gutter lights up with 5 new copper pins (Exec/Producer origin). The timeline strip shows hotspots at scenes 1, 14, 16, 21.** The beat ribbon's affected ticks pulse once.
5. Writer clicks first pin → page scrolls to scene 1, pin unfurls in the gutter with the producer's actual sentence, dated and quoted.
6. Writer presses `⌘K`, types `/structure tighten this opening`. Enter. A new pin appears below the producer note: three opening alternatives, Tab to cycle, Enter to accept.

**Why this is the demo:** it shows the workflow that doesn't exist anywhere else (ingest → structure → respond). 60 seconds, no hand-waving.

### Demo 2 — "Fix Benjamín's on-the-nose dialogue across the script"

1. Writer notices Benjamín's dialogue is bothering them. Selects one line: `Quéidiota eres.`
2. Right-click → `Find similar tics`. Or `⌘K` → `/find on the nose for benjamín`.
3. Tool: highlights 4 lines across 4 scenes. Timeline strip shows 4 amber ticks. Gutter shows 4 `Tic·B` pins.
4. Patterns topbar pill jumps from `0` to `1` with an "*on-the-nose · Benjamín*" tooltip.
5. Writer clicks first pin → scene 7 line: `Tenemos quehablarde Grupo Serrano.` Press `⌘R`. Three rewrites appear inline (none in red strike-through; the new line just appears below in the current revision color).
6. Tab through. Enter to accept. Old line moves to gutter as small "was:" pin. Pattern count drops to 3.

**Why this is the demo:** the pattern detection is the differentiator; the inline rewrite is the moment that visually demolishes the old diff-card paradigm.

### Demo 3 — "Hear Karla rehearse the scene"

1. Writer is on scene 7 with Karla's dialogue. `⌘K` → `/only karla`.
2. The page redraws. Karla's lines render normally; other characters' dialogue collapses to one-line stubs (cue-side script). Action lines dim to 25%.
3. `⌘K` → `/read karla`. Or `Shift+R`.
4. Karla's lines play in her assigned voice; other cues read by the Narrator in a softer track. The current word karaoke-highlights as it reads.
5. Writer hears the line `Si fuera por Benjamín, ya estaríaenterrada` and winces. Presses `Esc`. Cursor jumps to that line.
6. `⌘R`. Rewrite. Iterate.

**Why this is the demo:** rehearsal-mode reading exists nowhere else. Final Draft's read-aloud is monotonal and scene-only. SPLITDEV can own actor-side rehearsal.

## 6 — What ships, in what order

Three phases. Each is independently shippable.

### Phase A — Stop the bleeding (this week)

The bugs in §2. Nothing in §3 or beyond ships until these are fixed.

1. **Whitespace bug in the script renderer.** (§2.1) Single most damaging issue.
2. **Case-insensitive location grouping.** (§2.2)
3. **Beat ribbon overflow + edge padding.** (§2.3)
4. **Script clipped by ribbon, not bleeding under it.** (§2.4)
5. **Auto-reveal active scene in sidebar.** (§2.5)
6. **Kill `+ NEW NOTE` button — make right-click the canonical path.** (§2.6) Note empty state stays.
7. **Remove `★ Ask AI` from slug line.** (§2.7) If it survives, it lives in the command bar as `/ask` or as a selection verb.
8. **Realign the `S` margin pin to its anchor line.** (§2.8)
9. **Remove inline `— PAGE 3 —` divider; rely on margin page numbers.** (§2.9)
10. **Prompt for project title on first save.** (§2.10)

Total scope: 1 sprint. Net effect: the build stops embarrassing itself on a demo.

### Phase B — The architectural moves (next 2-4 weeks)

The big shifts from §4.

11. **Cursor in the page.** (§3.6) If not yet shipped: ship this first. Everything else depends on it.
12. **Slide-out sidebar (⌘1) + slide-out notes (⌘2) + solitude (⌘.).** (§4.1)
13. **Annotation gutter as the unified margin.** (§4.2) Replaces the floating `S` pin, the linked-note slug badge, the asterisks, the per-line origin marks.
14. **Command bar (⌘K) with the verbs in §4.3.**
15. **Inline rewrite (⌘R on selection, Tab to cycle, Enter to accept, old line moves to gutter).**
16. **Patterns pill in the topbar.** (§4.5)
17. **Reading mode keystroke (⌘\) + full-bleed paper.** (§3.10)

### Phase C — The differentiators (4-8 weeks)

The features that make SPLITDEV one-of-one.

18. **Lenses.** All six agents become page-margin annotators. Toggle from spine. (§4.4)
19. **Bible voice rules → active extractors on dialogue.** Copper margin diamonds for violations, citations on hover. (§3.5)
20. **Revision timeline strip.** Playhead, named milestones, scrubbable diff. (§4.6)
21. **Cards view as layer, not sibling.** (§3.9) `⌘\` toggle preserves underlying script state.
22. **Rehearsal-mode reading.** `/only <character>` + `/read <character>`. Cue-side script + voice. (§5, Demo 3)
23. **`INGEST NOTES` pipeline.** Paste email → parsed notes → pins. (§5, Demo 1)
24. **Agent prose voice.** Each agent writes distinctly. Horror like Shirley Jackson. Structure like Aristotle. Dialogue like Sorkin. (Currently they're all interchangeable; this is the biggest cheap upgrade in the entire backlog.)

## 7 — Visual language: what to tighten

Three quick observations on aesthetic. The build is mostly right.

- **Palette is good.** Copper accent, paper-tone neutrals, the muted blue for Blue Draft, the diagonal slate stripes — all earn the editorial-tool register.
- **Type is mostly right.** Courier for script, sans for chrome, italic display for project name. Drop JetBrains Mono entirely; Courier Prime is the only mono. Pick one sans (`Söhne` or `Inter Tight` or `GT Walsheim`) and never deviate.
- **Reduce origin-color count.** Producer / Exec / Director / Reader / Self / Table = 6 colors competing with 6 agent colors competing with 8 revision colors. Three palettes need to harmonize. Suggestion: agents own *high-chroma* (the lens dots, the in-text marks), origins own *low-chroma muted pigments* (the note pins), revisions own *the timeline pins* and never appear in body text. That gives each surface a single visual job.

## 8 — Closing — the one sentence

The current SPLITDEV is the **best-looking screenwriting app I've ever seen**, sitting on top of the **most ambitious feature model** in the category (note origins, patterns, agents, voice rules, multi-scene notes, ingest), held back by a **review-stance UI** that treats the page as one of three columns instead of as the canvas. Fix the bugs in §2 this week, ship the architecture in §4 next month, and you have a tool no working screenwriter will trade away.
